Computatrum is the long arc behind most of what I do in agents. The thesis:
the way to grow generally capable AI is to give it the same affordances a
human apprentice has — a computer with internet access, a screen, a
keyboard, a way to be corrected — and to let competence accumulate from
real use rather than from synthetic benchmarks.

What's hard:

- Credit assignment across long horizons of mostly-irrelevant pixels.
- Being teachable without being merely imitative.
- Surviving the long tail of "what do you mean by this dialog box."

What I've learned: the interesting bottleneck is not raw policy strength;
it's the substrate that lets a person sit next to the agent and quietly
correct it without breaking flow. Most "agent frameworks" haven't taken
this seriously.

Status: active research line, with a few small demos and a longer design
note in the works.
