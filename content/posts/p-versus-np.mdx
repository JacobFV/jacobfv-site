Bridging both the practical and the theoretical, permeating nearly every domain, a longstanding question of computer science stands the Problem. Succintly,

$$
\begin&#123;equation&#125;
\text&#123;Does P&#125; = \text&#123;NP?&#125;
\end&#123;equation&#125;
$$

where:
- $
\text&#123;P&#125; = \&#123; p \in \mathcal&#123;D&#125; \mid \exists a \in \mathcal&#123;A&#125; : \text&#123;a solves p in polynomial time&#125;\&#125;
$
- $
\text&#123;NP&#125; = \&#123; p \in \mathcal&#123;D&#125; \mid \exists a \in \mathcal&#123;A&#125; : \text&#123;a verifies a solution to p in polynomial time&#125;\&#125;
$
- $\mathcal&#123;D&#125;$ is the set of all decision problems
- $\mathcal&#123;A&#125;$ is the set of all algorithms

To solve it is not a mere exercise in curiosity, but -- as we will see -- transcendence beyond the very nature of provable nature of computation itself. Yet such is our aim.

Consider *this* proof $p_&#123;this&#125;$ and its implications: Either
$$
\begin&#123;equation&#125;
p_&#123;this&#125; \rightarrow \text&#123;P&#125;=\text&#123;NP&#125; \tag&#123;2&#125;
\end&#123;equation&#125;
$$

$$
\begin&#123;equation&#125;
p_&#123;this&#125; \rightarrow \text&#123;P&#125;\neq\text&#123;NP&#125; \tag&#123;3&#125;
\end&#123;equation&#125;
$$

or

$$
\begin&#123;equation&#125;
p_&#123;this&#125; \rightarrow \text&#123;P&#125;=\text&#123;NP&#125; \land \text&#123;P&#125;\neq\text&#123;NP&#125; \tag&#123;4&#125;
\end&#123;equation&#125;
$$

For convenience, let us define constants for $p_&#123;this&#125;$'s self-referential implication $p_&#123;\text&#123;P&#125;=\text&#123;NP&#125;&#125;$ \eqref&#123;eq:2&#125;, disproof $p_&#123;\text&#123;P&#125;\neq \text&#123;NP&#125;&#125;$ \eqref&#123;eq:3&#125;, and impossibility $p_&#123;\text&#123;P&#125;=\text&#123;NP&#125; \wedge \text&#123;P&#125;\ne\text&#123;NP&#125;&#125;$ \eqref&#123;eq:4&#125;. The latter is necesary to distinguish between provable and unprovable impossibilities, as we will see.

Now how shall we proceed? Abstractly, proof is the goal-directed application of implication rules $\mathcal&#123;R&#125;$ to a set of axioms $\mathcal&#123;S&#125;_0$.

1. Start by establishing our known axioms $\mathcal&#123;S&#125;_0 = \&#123; s_1, s_2, s_3, \dots, s_n \&#125;$,
2. Select an implication to apply $r_i \in \mathcal&#123;R&#125;$ to apply to $\mathcal&#123;S&#125;_0$,
3. Repeat, building a sequence of larger and larger axiom sets $\mathcal&#123;S&#125;_i = \mathcal&#123;S&#125;_&#123;i-1&#125; \cup \mathcal&#123;r&#125;(\mathcal&#123;S_&#123;i-1&#125;&#125;)$,
4. Stop when $\mathcal&#123;S&#125;_i$ contains a statement $s_n$ that we are trying to prove.

Note the distinction between the proof *process* and the final proof itself. The latter lends itself a direct step-by-step verification, whereas the former demands a graph search. Proof verification surely takes less time... Or does it?

The Cook-Levin theorom shows that if $\text&#123;P&#125;=\text&#123;NP&#125;$, then there must exist a polynomial running time complexity algorithm that solves graph search, an NP-Complete problem. As we have just seen, proof solving is a form of graph search. Thus, if $\text&#123;P&#125;=\text&#123;NP&#125;$, then the proof search algorithm most definitely runs in a countable number of steps with respect to the proof length, but depending on the value of $p_&#123;this&#125;$ the proof search algorithm may not.

If $\text&#123;P&#125;=\text&#123;NP&#125;$, then the proof search algorithm most definitely runs in a countable number of steps with respect to the proof length, but depending on the value of $p_&#123;this&#125;$ the proof search algorithm may not.

However Godel had more to say about unprovable proofs. His second incompleteness theorom states that no complete axiomatic system can prove its own consistency. $p_&#123;this&#125;$'s self-referential proof (right here) is most certainly a statement about its completeness. Therefore $p_&#123;this&#125;$ cannot be consistent. So we must also rule out both $p_&#123;\text&#123;P&#125;=\text&#123;NP&#125;&#125;$ and $p_&#123;\text&#123;P&#125;\ne\text&#123;NP&#125;&#125;$ as impossible.

Finally, consider $p_&#123;\text&#123;P&#125;=\text&#123;NP&#125; \wedge \text&#123;P&#125;\ne\text&#123;NP&#125;&#125;$: As discussed earlier, $p_&#123;this&#125;$'s existance necesitates that $M_p$ to halt.
